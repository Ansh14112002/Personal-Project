üî∞ Stock Market Basics

A stock (also known as equity) represents fractional ownership of a corporation. When you buy a company's stock, you're purchasing a small piece of that company, called a share.

Key concepts:

  • Common Stock: Grants voting rights and potential dividends
  • Preferred Stock: Typically has no voting rights but has higher claim on assets/earnings
  • Market Capitalization: Total value of all company shares (Share Price √ó Outstanding Shares)
  • Dividends: Periodic payments to shareholders from company profits

Stock markets are exchanges where buyers and sellers meet to trade shares of public companies. Major exchanges include:

NYSE (New York Stock Exchange)

World's largest stock exchange by market cap

NASDAQ

Electronic exchange known for tech companies

London Stock Exchange

Primary UK stock exchange

Tokyo Stock Exchange

Largest in Asia by market cap

Stock prices are determined by supply and demand dynamics in the market.

üìä Market Analysis Methods

Fundamental analysis evaluates securities by examining related economic, financial, and other qualitative and quantitative factors.

Key Financial Statements:

Balance Sheet

Shows assets, liabilities, and shareholders' equity at a point in time

Income Statement

Reports revenue, expenses, and profit over a period

Cash Flow Statement

Shows how cash enters and leaves the business

Key Metrics:

  • P/E Ratio: Price-to-Earnings ratio (Stock Price / EPS)
  • P/B Ratio: Price-to-Book ratio (Stock Price / Book Value per Share)
  • Debt-to-Equity: Measures financial leverage
  • ROE: Return on Equity (Net Income / Shareholder's Equity)
  • Current Ratio: Measures short-term liquidity

Technical analysis studies price movements and trading volume to identify patterns and trends.

Chart Types:

Line Charts

Simple closing price connections

Bar Charts

OHLC (Open-High-Low-Close) data

Candlestick Charts

Visual price action with color coding

Common Indicators:

Trend Indicators
  • Moving Averages (SMA, EMA)
  • MACD (Moving Average Convergence Divergence)
  • Parabolic SAR
Momentum Indicators
  • RSI (Relative Strength Index)
  • Stochastic Oscillator
  • CCI (Commodity Channel Index)
Volume Indicators
  • OBV (On-Balance Volume)
  • Volume Weighted Average Price (VWAP)
Volatility Indicators
  • Bollinger Bands
  • ATR (Average True Range)

Sentiment analysis gauges the market's emotional tone to predict price movements.

Sentiment Indicators:

  • Put/Call Ratio: Measures options market sentiment
  • VIX (Volatility Index): "Fear gauge" of the market
  • Advance/Decline Line: Breadth indicator showing market participation
  • Short Interest Ratio: Measures bearish sentiment
  • News Sentiment Analysis: AI-powered analysis of news tone

Contrarian Investing:

Many traders use sentiment indicators to identify potential market extremes - when everyone is bullish, it may signal a top, and vice versa.

‚ôüÔ∏è Trading Strategies

Value Investing

Popularized by Benjamin Graham and Warren Buffett, focuses on finding undervalued stocks trading below their intrinsic value.

Growth Investing

Targets companies expected to grow earnings at an above-average rate compared to the market.

Dividend Investing

Focuses on companies with strong histories of paying and increasing dividends.

Index Investing

Passive strategy of buying index funds that track market benchmarks.

Day Trading

Buying and selling securities within the same trading day to capitalize on short-term price movements.

Swing Trading

Holding positions for several days to weeks to capture short- to medium-term gains.

Scalping

Making dozens or hundreds of trades per day to profit from small price changes.

Algorithmic Trading

Using computer programs to execute trades based on predefined criteria at high speeds.

Basic Strategies

Covered Call

Selling call options against owned stock to generate income

Protective Put

Buying puts to protect against downside risk

Advanced Strategies

Straddle

Buying both call and put at same strike price

Strangle

Buying out-of-the-money call and put

Iron Condor

Combination of bull put and bear call spreads

Butterfly Spread

Combination of bull and bear spreads with three strike prices

Derivatives are financial contracts whose value is derived from an underlying asset.

Options

Contracts giving the right (but not obligation) to buy/sell an asset at a set price.

  • Calls: Right to buy
  • Puts: Right to sell
  • American vs. European: Exercise timing differences

Futures

Standardized contracts to buy/sell an asset at a future date and price.

  • Commodity Futures (oil, gold, wheat)
  • Financial Futures (index, interest rate)
  • Currency Futures

Other Derivatives

  • Swaps: Agreement to exchange cash flows
  • Warrants: Long-term options issued by companies
  • CFDs: Contracts for Difference (popular outside US)

üß† Market Psychology

Behavioral finance studies how psychological influences and biases affect financial behaviors.

Common Biases:

Cognitive Biases
  • Confirmation Bias
  • Anchoring
  • Hindsight Bias
  • Gambler's Fallacy
Emotional Biases
  • Loss Aversion
  • Overconfidence
  • Endowment Effect
  • Herding Behavior

Markets move in recurring cycles influenced by economic conditions and investor psychology.

Stages of Market Cycles:

1. Accumulation Phase

Informed investors begin buying when prices are low

2. Markup Phase

Prices begin rising, more investors participate

3. Distribution Phase

Smart money begins selling to latecomers

4. Markdown Phase

Prices decline as selling pressure increases

Economic Cycles:

  • Expansion: Growing economy, rising markets
  • Peak: Maximum economic activity
  • Contraction: Declining economic indicators
  • Trough: Bottom of economic activity

Effective risk management is crucial for long-term success in markets.

Key Principles:

  • Position Sizing: Never risk more than 1-2% of capital on single trade
  • Diversification: Spread risk across different assets/sectors
  • Stop-Loss Orders: Automatically exit losing positions
  • Risk-Reward Ratio: Aim for at least 1:2 (potential loss vs. gain)
  • Hedging: Using derivatives to offset potential losses

Common Mistakes:

  • Failing to cut losses quickly
  • Overtrading (too many positions)
  • Letting emotions drive decisions
  • Chasing "hot tips" without research
  • Lack of trading plan
